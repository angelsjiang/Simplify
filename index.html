Overview

Simplicity is a mobile application that simplifies your shop planning process. It presents all of the promotional deals from the grocery stores of your choice. It neatly categorizes items for easy searchability and sorts identical products by the prices offered by your favorite stores.

Simplicity also brings in a simple shopping list feature that allows you to add, remove, and check off items -- just like you do with pen and paper! Discounted items added to a list are grouped by stores, so you’ll never forget that one little thing after leaving the store.

Problem Statement

Printed grocery advertisements have been taking passive roles as information providers. They lie around, perhaps in a stack of similar advertisements from different stores. Together, they are maybe on a table, maybe in a bin, waiting for a chance to share the exciting deals they have to offer. That is, if their audience remembered to pick them up before they expire.

We are surrounded by a variety of groceries stores, each offering its best deals through its own platform. But, we are presented with the problem created by the dispersion of advertising information. How troublesome is it to price-match the items they need? On top of this problem, one undeniable reality is that our daily lives are often packed with all kinds of activities. Price-matching or remembering sales durations may require time and energy that we can hardly afford, causing inconvenience and introducing frustrations to our already busy lives.

To address this series of problems, we first define our essential goal as to develop a service that helps people save money on groceries. To do that, we conducted a series of research on experts and on potential users. In the following sections, we will discuss our methods and findings. Then, we will present the evolution of our design based on the evidence collected.

Audience

The main audience of Simplicity is grocery planners and shoppers. Whether you are making a list for others, making a list with others, or buying off a list from others, Simplicity is at your service.

By registering for a Simplicity account, the created lists are stored in a cloud database, making those lists available on any device. However, we understand that some may not want to open another account (and give themselves just another set of username and password to remember). As a solution, we also offer the service offline, in hope of helping people of different concerns and people in different circumstances.

Online or offline, Simplicity prompts you to select a list of their go-to stores. That way, the contents shown are always tailored to your preferences, and information from stores that you don’t go to will stay out of your way. Since the service only supports English and searches for your nearby stores using a U.S. zip code, this application is primarily assisting English speakers.

Since Simplicity is only available on smartphones, two assumptions that we have about our audience are: they are comfortable with navigating and operating technologies, and they have access to the internet.

Scope and Constraints


The scope of the Simplicity project includes:

  • Problem identification
  • Project management
  • Competitive analysis
  • User research and persona creation
  • User story design and wireframe construction
  • Usability testing and heuristic evaluation
  • High fidelity mockup construction
  • Final showcase and case study compilation
  • Appropriate deliverables to our stakeholders at the end of each process
There are two significant constraints: time and budget. From problem identification to the final howcase, the project spanned a total of three months, from March 29th to June 9th of 2021. Without a budget, the team relied solely on free online collaboration tools:
  • Google’s cloud-native tools (Drive, Docs, Slides, and Sheets) for content compilation
  • Figma with free Education licenses offered by UC Irvine for wireframe and prototype development
  • Trello for project management
  • Discord and Slack for team and stakeholder communications

Process

In this section, we will go through the research activities performed and the findings discovered.

Having decided to work on a solution to resolve our shop-planning frustrations, the team brainstormed on several problems that can be addressed. As a result of our brainstorming session, we identified possible user needs, our initial ideas, the activities necessary to accomplish our research objectives, and the final product we wish to deliver.

We hypothesized that shop-planning frustrations may be caused by the scatter of sales information from grocery stores. This sales information may be printed or digital, but both forms share a similarity: they are often presented in a passive manner. That is, they are lying there and waiting to be read. This presentation introduces some inconvenience to people, especially those whose daily schedule is often preoccupied by many activities. As a result, people need to actively search for and collect these sales information. In addition, they need to actively keep in mind the duration of specific sales in order to get the products they want at the price they desire. With those burdens, people tend to miss the sales information and the optimal shopping time. So, we want to help shoppers save money and relieve the stress built during this process.

With the problems discovered and the essence stated, we believed there could be a solution that combines both information collection and presentation in an active, unaggressive way. Our goals for our project are to ultimately effectively address this need and to assist users so they don’t miss the sale items they are interested in.

In our competitive analysis, we explored the market and discovered existing services that propose solutions to our overarching research goal. We looked at direct and indirect competitors. We learned about their target audience, the unique services that they offer, the things that they are doing right, and the things they are not doing right. In this section, we will briefly compare three competitors, two of which are direct competitors and the other indirect.

To understand our audience, we developed an interview protocol and recruited nine participants of varying cultural and educational backgrounds. Asking open-ended and closed-ended questions, we learned about their personal experiences on both the planning and the shopping process. When we shared our findings among the team, we realized surprisingly many commonalities in practice between the participants, despite their diverse backgrounds.
Some reappearing factors that shaped the interviewees’ shopping habits include store distance, store specialities, weekly sales, and product prices. Of the twenty groups created during the construction of the affinity diagram, there were several that confirmed our hypotheses raised in the project proposal. They are:

  • I don’t read physical advertisements
  • Sales are very important to me
  • I don’t actively look for sales information
Other popular opinions among the interviewees that will affect our product design include:
  • I use the built-in notes application on my phone to make my shopping list
  • I plan what to buy before my trip, though it may not take a physical form
  • I don’t usually use tools in my grocery shopping process
  • I change my plans depending on what is on sale
  • I use the store’s website or mobile application to look up sales information
  • I am convinced to buy the products that are on sale

     DEMOGRAPHICS & EXPERIENCES     
All but one of our interviewees are young adults at the age range of 20 to 30 years old. Having only one interviewee in her 50s may cause our results to be heavily skewed towards the younger demographic.
The academic background of our interviewees includes: user experience design, computer science, biological science, economy, and more. All of the interviewees had an occupation at the time when the interviews were conducted, and some had plans to further develop their academic careers.
The gender ratio in our interview is roughly 1 to 1; we interviewed four females and five males. We also asked about their families as we expected their answers to affect their shopping habits.
While none of our interviewees has kids, we found from both our lives and from research done on parents’ shopping behaviors (https://www.fluentco.com/blog/moms-shopping-habits/), which differs from the data that we collected. The research proved that being a parent, or more specifically a mother, largely impacts one’s lifestyle. Therefore, this is an important factor in diversifying opinions and feedback on information gathering and grocery shopping experiences. We will be taking parenthood into consideration in our product design.


     SHOPPING PLANS & ITEMS PURCHASED     
Four out of nine interviewees reported that they usually plan their shopping lists before they head to the grocery store. However, it is important to emphasize that three of those four also mentioned that they often purchase products that are not on their shopping lists.
While most of our interviewees expressed that the price of the products will affect their decisions, how they react to prices can be divided into three groups:
  • I change my plans depending on what’s on sale
  • My shopping list is heavily influenced by the items on sale
  • I price-match products based on my knowledge and experience


     THE USE OF TOOLS     
Four out of nine interviewees mentioned that they use the built-in notes application on their phone to build their shopping list, although one of the four specified that he only uses it when there are too many things to buy.
Six out of nine interviewees reported that they do not read physical advertisements. While one of the six interviewees explained that it is because she rarely receives them, the others thought negatively of physical advertisements.
Four interviewees, three of whom do not read physical advertisements, mentioned that they use the store’s websites or mobile applications to look up sales information. Two interviewees mentioned that they have tried third-party services before, but they no longer use them because of varying pain points.

We created four personas that capture the interview data collected. For each persona, we described the fictional character’s age, gender, relationship status, academic background, occupation, and personal experiences.
